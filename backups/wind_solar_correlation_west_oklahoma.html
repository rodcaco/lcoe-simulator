Methodology & assumptions

This tool models yearly-averaged hourly capacity factor profiles for wind and solar resources in the western Oklahoma / panhandle corridor. Profiles are synthetic approximations calibrated to published empirical data — they are not raw hourly timeseries from any single year or site. Users should treat outputs as directional for portfolio sizing and complementarity analysis, not as bankable energy yield estimates.

Wind diurnal profile

The 24-hour wind shape vector is derived from the diurnal pattern characteristic of the U.S. Southern Great Plains low-level jet (LLJ), where hub-height winds peak overnight (~22:00–04:00 CST) and trough midday (~12:00–15:00 CST). This pattern is well-documented across the Oklahoma panhandle, West Texas, and western Kansas corridors. The shape is normalized and then scaled to the user-selected peak capacity factor.

Default peak CF of 53% is based on the DOE Plains & Eastern Clean Line levelized cost analysis assumption for Oklahoma panhandle wind resources.1 The 30–45% statewide range reflects fleet-wide averages inclusive of older, shorter turbines.2 Modern turbines at 140m+ hub heights in the panhandle corridor can achieve 48–55% net CF.3

Solar diurnal profile

Solar CF is modeled as a Gaussian curve centered at 13:00 CST (accounting for Oklahoma's position within the Central time zone) with a standard deviation of 4.5 hours, truncated to zero before 06:00 and after 20:00. This approximates the yearly-averaged diurnal solar irradiance envelope for a fixed-tilt utility-scale PV system at ~36.5°N latitude.

Default peak CF of 25% reflects western Oklahoma GHI of ~5.0–5.5 kWh/m²/day,4 yielding annual CFs in the 22–26% range for fixed-tilt PV. Single-axis tracking would increase this to ~27–32%.

Correlation methodology

Pearson correlation coefficient (r) is computed across the 24 hourly wind and solar CF values. This captures the average diurnal complementarity only. Actual hourly correlations on any given day will vary significantly due to weather — frontal systems, cloud cover, and wind drought events can produce positive correlations (both resources low simultaneously). Published research shows hourly wind-solar correlations are weakest (near zero or weakly negative), while monthly/seasonal correlations are more strongly negative.5, 6

DC load assumption

The DC load line is set at 40% of total combined nameplate capacity (wind + solar). This is a rough proxy representing a flat 24/7 data center load sized to approximately match annual energy production from the renewable portfolio. In practice, DC loads are constant (not time-varying), and actual load sizing would be determined by interconnection capacity, not renewable nameplate.

Key limitations

  • Profiles are yearly averages — seasonal variation (stronger wind in spring, weaker in summer; stronger solar in summer) is smoothed out
  • Multi-day wind/solar drought events (3–7+ day calm/cloudy periods) are not represented in averaged profiles
  • No curtailment, transmission constraints, or wake losses modeled
  • No battery storage or gas backup dispatch modeled
  • Solar profile assumes fixed-tilt; single-axis tracking would widen and flatten the curve
  • Wind profile does not account for site-specific terrain, turbine model power curves, or interannual variability
